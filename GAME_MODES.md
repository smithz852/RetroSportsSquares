# Game Modes

Every Sports Squares game uses the same board: claim squares, digits are assigned, and each period of the real game (quarters or halves) crowns the square matching the score. What changes between modes is **what happens to the coins** — especially when a period lands on a square nobody claimed.

## How payouts work (all modes)

- **The pool** is everything wagered: price per square × squares claimed.
- The pool is split **evenly across the periods** of the game (a 4-quarter game with a 100-coin pool means 25 coins ride on each quarter).
- A period that lands on an **unclaimed square** has no winner — each mode gives that moment its own twist, from a harmless refund to an arrow in the back.
- **Coins move when the game ends.** Winnings shown during play are provisional; everything is tallied and paid out at final settlement.
- **Every coin wagered is paid back out.** The house keeps nothing. If *no* period all game has a winner, everyone simply gets their wager back — in every mode.
- Coins apply to **public games**. Private games play by the same rules for bragging rights, with no coin balance at stake.

---

## DEFAULT

*The classic. Steady payouts, no surprises.*

- Each period pays an equal share of the pool to its winner.
- A period with no winner pays nothing — its share is **returned to all players** at the end of the game, in proportion to what each player wagered.

**Example** — 100-coin pool, 4 quarters (25 each): you win Q1 and Q3 → you collect 50. Q4 lands unclaimed → at game end, its 25 coins are refunded across all players based on their wagers (winners included — you funded that quarter too).

---

## FAIR

*Missed periods make every win worth more.*

- The pool is split across the periods that were **actually won**, not all periods.
- Every unclaimed period raises the payout of every winning period. Win twice, hold two shares.
- Nothing is refunded at the end — the winners absorb it all.

**Example** — 100-coin pool, 4 quarters: normally 25 per quarter. Q2 goes unclaimed → the pool splits across 3 winning quarters instead, and each pays **33.33**. If only one quarter is won all game, its winner takes the entire 100.

---

## PUSH

*Missed periods roll their coins onto the next prize.*

- Each period pays its equal share, but an unclaimed period **pushes its share onto the next period's prize**.
- Consecutive misses stack — the pot keeps growing until someone claims it.
- If the pot is still riding when the game ends (the final period went unclaimed), it goes to the **earliest winner** of the game.

**Example** — 100-coin pool, 4 quarters (25 each): Q1 and Q2 both go unclaimed → Q3's winner collects **75** (25 + 50 pushed). If instead Q4 is the one nobody claims, its 25 coins land on whoever won first.

---

## THIEF

*A missed period arms an arrow. The next winner takes it in the back.*
*(Needs games with 3+ periods.)*

- Payouts use the FAIR split: the pool divides across winning periods, so missed periods fatten every win.
- A period with no winner **arms an arrow**, fired by the most recent winner (the *shooter*). Back-to-back misses don't stack — one arrow, re-armed.
- The **next player to win a period is eliminated**: the shooter takes their entire winnings **and all of their squares**. Stolen squares can win again — for the shooter.
- **Self-hit:** if the shooter wins the period right after their own arrow, they shoot themselves — no elimination, but their winnings are knocked loose as a **bounty the next winner collects**.
- **Arrow at the buzzer:** if the game ends with an arrow still in flight, it targets the **earliest surviving winner**. If that player is the shooter themselves, the arrow is a **dud** — it never seeks another target, and a self-hit on the final period is a dud too. An arrow never helps nor hurts its own shooter; at worst it fizzles.
- An arrow before anyone has won is a dud (nobody fired it).

**Example** — 100-coin pool, 4 quarters: you win Q1. Q2 goes unclaimed — your arrow is armed. A rival wins Q3… and you eliminate them: their Q3 winnings are yours, and their squares now belong to you. When Q4 lands on one of those stolen squares, *you* win it.

---

## DESTRUCTION

*A missed period drops a bomb on the last winner's coins.*
*(Needs games with 3+ periods.)*

- Each period pays its equal share, like DEFAULT.
- A period with no winner is a **bomb**: the most recent winner **loses everything they've collected**, and those coins become loot for the **next player to win a period**. Nobody is eliminated — you keep your squares and can rebuild.
- A second bomb before anyone wins again finds an empty pot and fizzles. Bombing yourself back just means collecting your own coins.
- If the bomb has no later winner (it hit on the final period), the loot goes to the **first winner** of the game.
- **Salvage rights:** the bombs' own period shares are collected at game end by the **first winner** — a reward for winning early that they keep even if they were bombed along the way.
- A bomb before anyone has won is a dud (nothing to blow up).

**Example** — 100-coin pool, 4 quarters (25 each): you win Q1. Q2 is a bomb — your 25 coins are blasted loose. A rival wins Q3 and collects 50 (their 25 + your loot). You win Q4 for 25, and at game end you also collect the bomb quarter's 25 in salvage as the game's first winner.

---

## Quick comparison

| | Unclaimed period becomes… | Who benefits | Elimination? |
|---|---|---|---|
| **DEFAULT** | A refund for everyone | The whole table | No |
| **FAIR** | A bigger payout per win | Every winner | No |
| **PUSH** | A growing pot on the next period | The next winner | No |
| **THIEF** | An arrow at the next winner | The shooter | **Yes** — coins *and* squares |
| **DESTRUCTION** | A bomb on the last winner | The next winner (+ first-winner salvage) | No |
